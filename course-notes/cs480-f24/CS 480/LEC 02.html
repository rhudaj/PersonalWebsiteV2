Loss as a Measure of Performance

Expected loss

The average loss we would expect if we could evaluate our model on the entire probability distribution PPP﻿ of the data.

Since PPP﻿ is usually unknown, we can't compute this directly. The expected loss is more of an ideal measure—it's what we would aim to minimize if we had infinite data.

Empirical (Average) Loss

  • The average loss is calculated over the training data, (xi⃗,yi)∼i.i.dP(\vec{x_i}, y_i) \sim_{i.i.d} P(xi​​,yi​)∼i.i.d​P﻿, and approximates the expected loss.
  • we seek to minimize this; empirical loss minimization (ELM)

2.2 — Stability and Generalization

  • Why Large Weights are Bad

    Weights Becoming large Through Optimization:

    • Multicollinearity: When the features (columns of A) are highly correlated, the model might rely too much on certain features, leading to large weights.
    • Overfitting: If the model tries to fit the training data too perfectly (especially if there is noise), it may adjust the weights excessively, making some of them very large to capture small variations in the data.

    Large Weights ⇒ Instability

    • Sensitivity to Small Changes: the model sensitive to tiny variations or noise in the data (from your model being fit to noise in training data).

    ⇒ Poor Generalization

    • Overfitting: the model has learned the noise instead of the underlying pattern.
    • Poor Performance on Different Data
