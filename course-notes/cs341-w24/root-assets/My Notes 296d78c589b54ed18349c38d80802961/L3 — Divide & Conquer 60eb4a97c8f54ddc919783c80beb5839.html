L3 — Divide & Conquer

Notes on divide and conquer:

  • often helps to assume nnn﻿ is a power of 2
  • often need to show that the cases are mutually exclusive
    • i.e, left, right and transverse cases
  • not efficient when subproblem sizes are unbalanced

What

  1. Split a problem to subproblems
  1. Solve the subproblems (with the same algorithm)
  1. Use subproblem result to derive the final result.

Counting Inversions

Algorithm, V1

O(n⋅log⁡2n)O(n\cdot \log^2n)O(n⋅log2n)﻿

  • cLc_LcL​﻿ : inversions in left half
  • cRc_RcR​﻿ : inversions in right half
  • cTc_TcT​﻿ : (transverse) between both halves

Finding cTc_TcT​﻿

Sort both halves (does not change # inversions between them) → O(nlogn)O(nlogn)O(nlogn)﻿

For each item in LEFT, binary-search for its position in RIGHT

  • binary search: O(logn)O(logn)O(logn)﻿
  • total: O(nlogn)O(nlogn)O(nlogn)﻿

This would give a total runtime of O(n⋅log⁡2n)O(n\cdot \log^2n)O(n⋅log2n)﻿

Algorithm. V2

O(nlogn)O(nlogn)O(nlogn)﻿

Instead of first sorting and then counting inversions, we can do it at the same time, in linear time.

So we are counting inversion, while we merge the two arrays.

So this algorithm is merge-sort + counting inversions

Idea

  • i←i \larri←﻿indexing left half
  • j←j \larrj←﻿ indexing right half
  • we copy SSS﻿ into AAA﻿ to have our original array to index with iii﻿ and jjj﻿
  • we are sorting AAA﻿ as we traverse SSS﻿ and count inversions
